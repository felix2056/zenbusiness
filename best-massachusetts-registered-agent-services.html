What is a Massachusetts resident agent?

Every business entity in the state of Massachusetts is required to have a resident agent. In fact, you won’t be allowed to finish the formation process for your company until you designate your resident agent.

The most important aspect of this role is receiving document deliveries from the state government and process servers, then alerting your business of the delivery before forwarding the documents to you. The role of the resident agent is so important because it establishes a point of contact for your business with the state and others so that they have a reliable place to get in touch with an official representative of your company at any time.

Unlike some states, Massachusetts does not provide a comprehensive (or even a partial) list of resident agents serving in the state. You’ll have to do some of your own research to find the right agent for your business; thankfully, that’s where this guide comes in handy.

What does Massachusetts law say about resident agents?

Each state has its own rules and regulations surrounding resident agents. For the most part, these laws are fairly similar, but it’s always worth taking a look to see what Massachusetts requires.

In Massachusetts, we’ll need to take a look at the Business Corporations Chapter of the Massachusetts General Laws, which says:

  • Every entity that files with the Secretary of the Commonwealth (both domestic and foreign) must appoint a resident agent.
  • The agent may be an individual resident of Massachusetts or a corporation with the authority to operate in the state.
  • An agent must be continuously maintained.

All of the companies we discussed above meet these regulations and are options for Massachusetts resident agent service. When you’ve made your choice, you can indicate your agent’s name and address on your Certificate of Formation.

What about small, Massachusetts-only resident agent services?

As you’ve probably noticed, the resident agent services we’ve listed here serve businesses across the country. But if you Google “Massachusetts Resident Agent,” you’ll probably see results for local services, too. These can be local entrepreneurs with a knack for clerical tasks, offices dedicated to serving businesses in their counties, or statewide companies.

On one hand, using a local service can have its advantages. Some will be cheaper than nationwide firms (but not always!). And each state has its own nuances for business compliance requirements, Massachusetts included. A resident agent service that services Massachusetts alone may have detailed knowledge about those requirements. Granted, most national services do an excellent job of understanding local requirements. But they might not give you the same “homegrown Massachusetts” feeling as a local agent service.

That said, national businesses like ZenBusiness give you streamlined solutions. Let’s say you’re using a local Massachusetts-only resident agent service, but it’s time to add locations in Rhode Island. You could search for an additional agent that services Rhode Island businesses. But that’s going to require a lot of additional paperwork, including new receipts, new automatic payments, more emails…you get the idea. But if you’re using a national service, you can cut down the “new” paperwork and focus on what really matters: growing your business.

Ultimately, the choice is up to you, and there’s no “wrong” answer. The most important thing is that you appoint one so you can run a compliant Massachusetts business.

The role of a Massachusetts resident agent is critical to your company’s compliance and legal standing. FAQs About Massachusetts’ Best Resident Agent Services

  • In Massachusetts, there aren’t many complicated requirements for resident agent eligibility. Any individual or corporation authorized to conduct business in the state can serve as your company’s resident agent. The only requirements are that they have a permanent address in Massachusetts and that your company itself can’t serve as its own resident agent (although you or one of your co-owners may assume this role individually).

    Like most states, Massachusetts does not have an explicit age requirement (some states require the appointed agent to be at least 18 years of age). That said, we do recommend picking an individual that you trust or one of the services listed above. If the resident agent does not fulfill their duties completely and accurately, it can cause unnecessary hassle for a business. Most responsible adults can fill this role easily, but we do recommend careful consideration while you make this appointment.

  • Yes. If your resident agent is an individual, that person must live in Massachusetts. If your agent is a business entity, that company must either be a domestic Massachusetts business or one with the authority to conduct business in the state.

  • Massachusetts keeps the process of changing your resident agent pretty simple. All you have to do is download the Statement of Change of Registered Agent/Registered Office form, fill it out, and submit it to the Secretary of the Commonwealth. There is a $25 fee if you file with a paper form, but if you file online, it’s free. For more information, see our Massachusetts change of resident agent page.

  • If your resident agent chooses to resign and no longer serve as your agent, they will notify you and the Secretary of the Commonwealth by filing the Certificate of Resignation of Resident Agent form. The effective date for this form cannot be less than 30 days after the initial filing; that 30-day period allows you plenty of time to find a replacement.

  • Potentially nothing! If you file the Statement of Change form online, the filing fee is free. Filing with a paper form costs $25. Either way, it’s important to replace a resigned agent in a timely manner to avoid any additional penalties and fees. As long as you stay compliant with the requirement to continuously maintain an agent, you won’t have to pay more than the $25 filing fee for the Change of Registered Agent on a paper form. Since the form covers both the resignation of your old agent and the appointment of your new one, that’s the only expense you must pay.

  • One common option is to serve as your company’s own resident agent or enlist the assistance of a trusted friend or family member. The other route is to hire an attorney or accountant to handle this role. Maybe you prefer the added professionalism these people can provide, maybe you have an existing relationship with an attorney or accountant you trust, etc.
